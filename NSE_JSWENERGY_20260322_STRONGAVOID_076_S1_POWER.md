## 🌐 PHASE 0.5: THEMATIC ASSESSMENT (12 Points — Separate from 135-pt total)

### 0.5.1 Theme Identification & Definition — 2/2

**Theme in ONE sentence:** India's energy transition from fossil fuel dominance to a diversified renewable energy mix, targeting 500 GW of non-fossil fuel capacity by 2030 under national climate commitments.

**Structural driver:** Government-mandated Renewable Purchase Obligations (RPOs), COP26/G20 net-zero commitments, surging peak power demand (driven by air conditioning penetration, data centers, EV charging), and declining RE tariffs making renewables cheaper than new thermal.

**Cyclical or secular:** Secular — energy demand growth is structural (GDP-linked), and the shift to renewables is policy-mandated and economically driven.

🔒 **Score: 2/2** | Source: Ministry of Power/MNRE targets, COP26 commitments

### 0.5.2 Theme Maturity & Penetration — 3/3

**Current penetration:** RE accounted for ~18% of India's total electricity generated in Q2 FY26 (up from 15% same period prior year). Installed RE capacity ~200 GW against 500 GW target by 2030.

**Organized share:** Top 5 IPPs (NTPC, Adani Green, Tata Power, JSW Energy, Torrent Power) collectively hold <30% of total generation capacity. Massive fragmented opportunity.

**Global benchmark:** China reached ~35% RE generation share in 2024; EU at ~44%. India at 18% is a decade behind.

**Runway remaining:** 300 GW of RE capacity additions needed by 2030; further 200+ GW by 2035 under extended targets. 10+ years of structural runway.

🔒 **Score: 3/3** | Source: CEA data, IEA World Energy Outlook

### 0.5.3 Global Precedent Check — 1/2

**Global market:** China's energy transition — from 2015-2024, China added ~500 GW of RE capacity, led by state-backed utilities and private developers. Consolidation favored top 5-10 players nationally.

**Enabling conditions in India:** PLI for solar manufacturing, RPO mandates, Green Open Access rules, sovereign green bonds. Partially present.

**KEY DIFFERENCES (mandatory):**
1. India's grid infrastructure is significantly weaker than China's — transmission bottlenecks cause RE curtailment risk
2. India's discom financial health is far worse than Chinese state utilities — payment risk is structural
3. India's land acquisition challenges are more severe — no centralized state-backed land pooling
4. India lacks China's domestic equipment manufacturing dominance — import dependency on solar cells/wafers

These differences are material and reduce the probability of identical consolidation outcomes. Score reduced from 2 to 1.

🔒 **Score: 1/2** | Source: IEA, CEA, State discom reports

### 0.5.4 Policy & Regulatory Tailwind — 2/2

**Specific policies:**
- 500 GW non-fossil fuel target by 2030 (PM commitment at COP26)
- RPO mandate increasing to 43% by FY30 (MNRE notification)
- PLI for solar manufacturing: ₹24,000 Cr allocation
- BESS viability gap funding: ₹3,760 Cr
- Green hydrogen mission: ₹19,744 Cr
- Offshore wind target: 37 GW by 2030

**Risk of reversal:** Low — cross-party consensus on energy transition; economic rationale (RE tariffs < ₹3/unit vs new thermal ₹5+/unit) removes political risk.

🔒 **Score: 2/2** | Source: MNRE, Union Budget 2025-26

### 0.5.5 Competitive Landscape Evolution — 1/1

**Top players:** NTPC (~76 GW), Adani Green (~31 GW RE), JSW Energy (~13.3 GW), Tata Power (~14 GW), Torrent Power (~6 GW).

**Trend:** Consolidating. JSW acquired O2 Power (4.7 GW) and KSK Mahanadi (1.8 GW) in FY25. Barriers to entry rising — land, transmission connectivity, balance sheet strength required for scale. Smaller players being acquired.

🔒 **Score: 1/1** | Source: Company filings, ICRA

### 0.5.6 Theme Risk Assessment — 1/2

**What kills or stalls the theme:**
1. Grid curtailment risk — RE additions outpacing transmission infrastructure (already visible in some states)
2. Discom payment delays — receivables risk for RE developers
3. Policy uncertainty on thermal — if government reverses on thermal phase-down, hybrid economics change
4. Interest rate environment — RE projects are capital-intensive; rising rates compress returns

**Probability within investment horizon:** Grid curtailment risk is moderate (20-30% probability of becoming material within 3-5 years). Discom payment risk is ongoing but manageable for top-tier players.

**Early warning signal:** Increase in RE curtailment percentages above 5% nationally; discom receivable days exceeding 120 days.

🔒 **Score: 1/2** | Source: ICRA sector reports, CEA data

## PHASE 1: BUSINESS ORIENTATION (20 Points)

### 1.1 Business Model Clarity — 4/5

JSW Energy is an integrated power generation company with diversified fuel sources. As of Dec 2025, total installed capacity is 13.3 GW comprising:
- **Thermal:** ~5,658 MW (Ratnagiri 1,200 MW, Barmer 1,080 MW, Vijayanagar 860 MW, Utkal 350 MW, Nandyal 18 MW, Salboni under construction, KSK Mahanadi 1,800 MW)
- **Hydro:** ~1,391 MW (Baspa 300 MW, Karcham Wangtoo 1,091 MW) + Kutehr 240 MW commissioned
- **Wind:** ~2,543 MW (including O2 Power assets)
- **Solar:** ~675 MW (organic + O2 Power)
- **Other:** Power trading, transmission (JV with MSETCL), green hydrogen (Vijayanagar)

Revenue model: Long-term PPAs (95% of capacity tied up) providing availability-linked capacity charges (thermal/hydro) and fixed tariff income (RE). Merchant exposure at 8%, declining to <5% by Apr 2026.

Segment split: Thermal ~63% of revenue (H1 FY25), renewables growing rapidly post-O2 Power acquisition.

Monetization mechanism explicit. Complex subsidiary structure (50+ entities) is a minor negative.

🔒 **Score: 4/5** | Source: Screener.in, Q2 FY26 concall, ICRA
